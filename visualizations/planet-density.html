Densities of the Terrestrial Planets

  • How can we find the size of a planet's metal core? We can't dig down to the core! We must use indirect means. Density is a clue: metal is denser than rock.
  • Calculated Density of a planetary body is determined from a chosen mixture of rock and metal. In reality, rock and metal have a range of densities which depend on pressure, chemical composition, and other factors.
  • For this simplified simulation, the metallic core of a body has a density of 8000 kg per cubic meter (or 8 grams per cubic centimeter) while the rocky mantle and crust of the body has a density of 3000 kg per cubic meter (or 3 grams per cubic centimeter).
  • Measured Density of a planetary body is determined from the body's observed mass and radius. By calculating the pressure on the deep interior of a planetary body, the uncompressed density of the body can be determined. This density most closely agrees with the densities for rock and metal given above.
  • To begin your exploration, choose a terrestrial body from the navigation bar on the left.
  • Use the slider below to change the relative amount of metal and rock filling the interior of the body.
  • Adjust the fraction of metal in the body to match the Calculated Density to the Measured Density.
  • A match between these densities reveals the approximate size of the body's metallic core.
